Session Info
6 Scripts Choose the approach matching your contact source
1
Neighbourhood Canvass (Door Knock)
Buyer in the area · Systematic street work
▼
When to use: You are systematically working a street or neighbourhood. No specific prior knowledge of this property's history.
"Good morning — my name is Peter Luo, I'm a real estate agent with RE/MAX Realtron. I work a lot in this neighbourhood, and I'm always looking to connect with homeowners here."
"I actually have a buyer who is specifically looking in this area — they've been quite particular about the street, so I'm going door to door to see if anyone might be open to a conversation."
"I'm not here to pitch you on anything — I'm just curious: is selling something you've ever thought about, even casually?"
〔 If yes → bridge into EER objection handling. If no → ask for referrals and leave card. 〕
"If not, that's completely fine — I appreciate you answering the door. Would you happen to know any neighbours who've mentioned thinking about making a move?"
2
Property Sign Observed (No Longer Active)
You saw a sign that has since been removed
▼
When to use: You observed a real estate sign that has since been removed and are following up based on that public observation.
"Hi — my name is Peter Luo with RE/MAX Realtron. I was in the neighbourhood a little while ago and noticed there had been a sign in front of your home at one point."
"I wanted to reach out because I work with buyers who are actively looking in this area, and I thought it might be worth a quick conversation — just to see if selling is still something that might be on your mind."
"I'm not here to pressure you in any direction. I'm just someone who works in this neighbourhood and likes to keep things simple. Would you be open to a short conversation?"
〔 The sign observation is the contact basis — do not reference any board data. Keep it low-pressure. 〕
3
Referral from Neighbour or Mutual Contact
Someone you know mentioned them
▼
When to use: A neighbour, mutual acquaintance, or past client mentioned that this homeowner may be thinking about selling.
"Hi [Name] — my name is Peter Luo, I'm a real estate agent with RE/MAX Realtron. I hope it's okay to reach out — [referral name] mentioned that you might be going through some changes and had been thinking about the house."
"I don't want to assume anything, and I'm not here with any agenda. I just thought — if there's ever a moment where selling is on your mind — I'd rather you have someone you can call who actually knows this neighbourhood, rather than going in cold."
"Is that a conversation that would be worth five minutes right now, or would another time work better?"
〔 Always confirm the referral contact is comfortable with being named. Keep the tone warm and unhurried. 〕
4
Follow-Up to a Value Report or Mailer
You sent a market update — following up now
▼
When to use: You sent a market update, home valuation offer, or neighbourhood report to homes in the area and are following up on that mailing.
"Hi — this is Peter Luo calling from RE/MAX Realtron. I sent a neighbourhood market update to homes on your street a little while ago, and I wanted to follow up in case you had a chance to look at it."
"I don't know if selling is on your radar at all — but the reason I send these is because a lot of homeowners in this neighbourhood don't realize what the market has been doing, and I'd hate for you to make any big decisions without having accurate information."
"Even if you're not thinking about selling right now, is it a conversation worth having — just so you know where you stand?"
〔 This approach works well as a phone call. Reference the specific mailer to establish context and credibility. 〕
5
Community or Social Connection
School, gym, community group — they know your face
▼
When to use: You have a connection through a community group, local business, school, or social network — and they may know you by name or face.
"Hey [Name] — it's Peter Luo. We've run into each other a few times at [context — school, gym, community group]. I hope it's okay that I'm reaching out like this."
"I've been thinking about reaching out for a while, actually — I didn't want to bring work into things, but someone in the neighbourhood mentioned you might be thinking about the house, and I figured I'd rather you hear it from someone you know than get a cold call from a stranger."
"I'm honestly not here with a pitch. I just want you to know I'm around if it ever becomes a conversation worth having. Would that be okay?"
〔 Keep the tone personal and warm. Do not lead with a market pitch — lead with the relationship. 〕
6
Phone Call — Area Prospecting
Public directory · No prior knowledge of property
▼
When to use: Calling homeowners in a specific area from a publicly available directory. You have no specific knowledge of this property's history.
"Good morning — my name is Peter Luo, I'm a real estate agent with RE/MAX Realtron. I know this is a bit out of the blue, and I'll be brief."
"I'm working with a few buyers who are very specifically looking in [neighbourhood name], and I'm calling homeowners directly because sometimes the right home never makes it to the market — it gets matched before that."
"I'm not calling to sell you anything or sign you up for anything. I just want to ask one question: is selling something that's been on your mind at all, even as a long-term thought?"
〔 Keep it under 30 seconds to the question. If they say no, thank them and ask for referrals. If yes, bridge into EER. 〕
"If not, I completely understand — and I'm sorry to interrupt your morning. Do you happen to know anyone in the area who's been talking about making a move? That's really all I'm after."
📌 How to Use
Always complete Empathy before Explore. Always complete Explore before Respond. The Response must reference what they actually told you — not a generic pitch.

1
"We're not selling anymore."
Protective reaction — often not permanent
▼
The seller has stepped back from selling entirely. Usually frustration or fatigue — not a final decision.
Underlying Reasons
1Frustration and emotional exhaustion from the previous listing attempt
2Disappointment with results — low showings, no offers, or a low offer that didn't meet expectations
3Dissatisfaction with the previous agent's effort or communication
4Market concern — they believe conditions have worsened and don't see the point
5Personal circumstances changed — motivation to sell has faded
6Fear of repeating the same outcome with a new agent
7Exploring alternatives — renting out, staying longer, or waiting for a better market
Phase 1 — Empathy
Acknowledge
Mirror their position back
"I hear you — you've decided to step back from selling for now."
Validate
Normalise the decision
"After going through the listing process and not getting the result you were hoping for, that makes complete sense. A lot of people feel the same way."
Bridge
Signal curiosity, not pitch
"I'm not here to talk you into anything. I'm just curious — if it's okay — what led to that decision?"
Phase 2 — Explore
Explore
Open-ended — find the real reason
"Was it something about the process itself, the offers you received, the agent experience — or something else entirely?"
〔 Listen fully. The real reason usually comes in the second or third sentence. Do not rush to respond. 〕
Appreciate
Acknowledge specifically
"Thank you for telling me that — I appreciate you being straight with me. That really helps."
Isolate
Confirm this is the main issue
"Is that the main thing — [their reason] — or is there something else sitting underneath it?"
⚠️ De-escalation — if they resist questions
"No problem at all — I'm not here to dig into anything you'd rather not revisit. Would it be okay if I just left you a bit of information on what's been happening in the area? You can do whatever you like with it."
Phase 3 — Respond
Reframe
From "failed listing" → "information gap"
"A lot of sellers who step back after an expired listing tell me the same thing later: the missing piece wasn't the house — it was the strategy or the timing. The house didn't change. What happened around it did."
Respond
Tailor to what they told you in Explore
If offers were too low: "If the offers were below what you needed, that often means the pricing or the marketing didn't attract the right buyers — not that the number isn't achievable. I'd want to show you specifically what I think happened and what I'd do differently."
Confirm
Check if it landed
"Does that make sense for your situation, or is there something I'm missing?"
Transition
Low-commitment next step
"I'm not asking you to do anything today. Would it be worth 30 minutes for me to come by, show you what I think happened, and leave you with a clear picture — no obligation at all?"

"Our previous agent couldn't sell it."
Still wants to sell — frustrated with the agent
▼
An opening — they're still interested in selling but frustrated. Do not defend the industry.
Underlying Reasons

2Insufficient or ineffective marketing — poor photos, limited online exposure, no targeting
3Weak communication — infrequent updates left the seller feeling ignored
4Lack of buyer feedback — seller never knew what buyers actually thought
5No pricing strategy adjustment when the market signalled the price was off
6Mismatch between agent expertise and property type or price range
Phase 1 — Empathy
Acknowledge
Receive — don't defend the industry
"I hear that — the property was listed and didn't sell."
Validate
Name the frustration
"That's genuinely frustrating — you went through the whole process, and at the end of it, you're back where you started."
Bridge
Show curiosity, not judgement
"I'd love to understand what happened, if you're open to sharing — not to point fingers, just so I'm not walking in blind."
Phase 2 — Explore
Explore
Invite the story
"What was the experience like? What do you think went wrong — or what were you most frustrated by?"
Appreciate
Reflect their specific words back
"That's really helpful — thank you. It sounds like [reflect their specific words back]."
Isolate
Is there more?
"Is the agent's performance the main issue, or is there something else — pricing, the market, timing — that's also on your mind?"
Phase 3 — Respond
Reframe
Separate the property from the experience
"The property usually isn't the problem. Most expired listings come down to one of three things — pricing, exposure, or strategy. And all three are fixable."
Respond
Reference what they told you — be specific
If communication was the issue: "The way I work is structured around communication — I commit to a written update every week regardless of activity, so you're never wondering what's happening. You always have something in writing."
Confirm
Verify alignment
"Does that address what frustrated you, or is there more to it?"
Transition
Propose the visit
"What I'd suggest is coming by for about 30–45 minutes — I'll bring a full breakdown of what I think happened and what I'd do differently. No agenda other than leaving you better informed."

Loyalty — often emotional, not logical
▼

Underlying Reasons
1Genuine loyalty and personal relationship — they like the agent
2The agent has promised to try harder, adjust price, or change strategy
3Fear of change — switching agents feels like starting over
4Discomfort with having to fire someone they know
5Belief that the market, not the agent, was the real problem
Phase 1 — Empathy
Acknowledge
Respect the loyalty — don't challenge it
"I understand — you're planning to relist with the same agent."
Validate
Validate the reasoning
"That makes sense — you have a relationship with them, and starting over with someone new can feel like a lot."
Bridge
Keep the door open
"I'm not here to change your mind. I'd just ask one thing — would you be open to a quick conversation before you sign anything, just so you have a basis for comparison?"
Phase 2 — Explore
Explore
Understand the loyalty
"What is it about going back to them that feels right — is it the relationship, a specific plan they've laid out, or just familiarity with the process?"
Appreciate

▼

Underlying Reasons

2Genuine concern about current buyer demand or market conditions
3Using timing as a way to delay without fully deciding

Phase 1 — Empathy
Acknowledge

Acknowledge the logic

Phase 2 — Explore
Explore

Reframe

Use local data — not national headlines
If waiting for rates to drop: "When rates do drop significantly, it typically brings a surge of buyers — and a surge of sellers. Acknowledge
Receive without defending
"That's a fair question to ask."

Explore

"I'm not interested."
Surface-level reflex — don't push through it
▼
Work around it, not through it. One question, one small offer, then a graceful exit. The follow-up touchpoint may do more than any continued conversation today.
Underlying Reasons
1Overwhelmed by agent contact after the listing ended
2Emotionally exhausted from the previous experience
3Genuinely decided not to sell — at least for now
4Using it as a polite brush-off to avoid a sales call
5Personal circumstances have changed
Phase 1 — Empathy
Acknowledge
Receive without resistance
"That's completely fine — I appreciate you being straight with me."
Validate
Reduce pressure immediately
"I'm not here to convince you of anything. I know you've probably heard from a lot of people lately."
Bridge
One question before you go
"Can I ask you one quick thing — just to make sure if things ever change, I have a sense of your situation?"

Explore
One focused, non-threatening question
"Is it more that you've decided not to sell at all — or that you're just not ready to think about it yet?"

Appreciate
Acknowledge their answer

Reframe
Only if there's an opening — don't push otherwise
